# 2. Analytical Methods

## 2.1 Net Load Analysis  
Net Load indicates whether the home currently requires imported energy (from grid/battery) or has excess solar power.

**Formula:**  
Net Load (kW) = Home Load (kW) − Solar Output (kW)

- Net Load > 0 → Energy deficit  
- Net Load < 0 → Solar surplus  

This metric is foundational in household power flow analysis.

---

## 2.2 Solar Utilization Rate  
Measures how efficiently solar generation is directly consumed by the home.

**Formula:**  
Solar Utilization = min(Solar Output, Home Load) ÷ Solar Output

- High utilization → solar is efficiently used onsite  
- Low utilization → solar energy is being wasted  
- Undefined when Solar Output = 0 (treated as NaN)

---

## 2.3 Battery SOC Trend Analysis  
The battery SOC is simulated as a linear increase from  
**battery_level − 10% → battery_level** across 24 hours.

Approximate charging rate:

10% ÷ 24 hours ≈ **0.42% per hour**

This allows interpretation of charging behavior under typical solar/load profiles.

---

## 2.4 EV Charging Feasibility  
EV charging power: **7.2 kW**  

We evaluate whether solar surplus can support EV charging:

**Surplus Solar** = max(0, Solar Output − Home Load)  
**EV Charging Deficit** = 7.2 − Surplus Solar

- Low deficit → favorable EV charging conditions  
- High deficit → charging should be delayed  

This aligns with the dashboard’s recommendation logic.

# 4. Interpretation of Results

1. **Solar Surplus Behavior**  
   - Midday solar significantly exceeds home load  
   - Enables EV charging and battery charging without grid usage  

2. **Energy Deficit Periods**  
   - Occur during early morning and late evening  
   - Require either grid power or battery discharge  

3. **Solar Utilization**  
   - A utilization rate of **65.7%** is realistic and represents healthy renewable use  
   - Excess solar could be further stored or shifted via load management  

4. **Battery Trend**  
   - SOC increases gradually, reflecting mild charging behavior  
   - Suggests no aggressive demand on the battery  

5. **EV Charging Feasibility**  
   - During 16 hours of solar surplus, EV charging would be cost-effective  
   - Remaining hours should delay charging or rely on stored energy

# 5. Limitations

To maintain clarity and reproducibility:

- Solar modeled using a sinusoidal daylight curve  
- Home load generated uniformly between 2–5 kW  
- Battery SOC modeled linearly  
- EV charging logic is rule-based, not optimization-driven  
- No real-world weather, tariff, or hardware integration  

These constraints are appropriate for a UI/analysis prototype.
